When someone is very ill or has died, families are often asked to make meaningful decisions while they are tired and grieving. These guides offer a calmer place to begin. They cover recording a loved one's voice when time is short, writing a eulogy that sounds like the person, creating a tribute or memorial book, planning a meaningful celebration of life, and deciding what to do with letters, journals, photographs, and belongings.

A good tribute is not a list of dates or achievements. It holds the habits, sayings, contradictions, jokes, recipes, and small acts of care that made the person recognizable. You do not need to capture everything. A few specific stories can carry more truth than a page of formal language.

Use what helps and leave what does not. The goal is not a perfect memorial; it is an honest one that gives family and friends something real to remember.
