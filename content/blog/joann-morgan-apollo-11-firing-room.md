*Published July 9, 2026 · Updated July 9, 2026*

The only woman in the Apollo 11 firing room at liftoff was JoAnn Morgan, an instrumentation controller for NASA who grew up in Titusville, Florida. On July 16, 1969, as Apollo 11 launched humans toward the moon, she was the sole woman among the engineers in Kennedy Space Center's Launch Control Center. Here's the story of a Space Coast trailblazer.

Picture the firing room at Kennedy Space Center on the morning of July 16, 1969. Rows of engineers at their consoles, the countdown ticking, the most important launch in history seconds away. Scan that room full of men and you'll find one woman at her station, doing her job.

That was JoAnn Morgan. And she was one of ours, a Titusville girl who helped send people to the moon.

## The short version

- **Grew up in Titusville, Florida**, moving there as a high school junior
- **Age 17** — Begins summer work as a trainee at Cape Canaveral
- **1960s** — Becomes a measurement and instrumentation engineer at Kennedy Space Center; works on Mercury, Gemini, and Apollo
- **July 16, 1969** — Serves as instrumentation controller for the Apollo 11 launch, the only woman in the firing room at liftoff
- **1970s–90s** — Breaks barrier after barrier: first woman division chief, first woman senior executive at KSC
- **1995** — Inducted into the Florida Women's Hall of Fame
- **2003** — Retires from NASA after decades of service

## Three turning points

**A summer job at the Cape.** At just 17, Morgan took a summer trainee position at what is now Cape Canaveral, right in her Space Coast backyard. It was the doorway to everything. That early foot in the door, at the exact place and moment the space program was taking off, set the course of her entire life.

**Earning her place in the firing room.** Being the only woman in a room of men in 1969 was not automatic or easy. Morgan was there because she was good, and because a supervisor, Karl Sendler, advocated for her to be in the room during the Apollo 11 launch. She took her seat at the instrumentation console and did the work while history happened around her. She belonged there, and she proved it.

**Becoming a first, again and again.** Morgan didn't stop at Apollo 11. Over the following decades she became the first woman to win a NASA Sloan Fellowship, the first woman division chief at Kennedy Space Center, and its first woman senior executive. Each title she earned widened the door for the women who came after her.

## How the Space Coast shaped her

This is a Space Coast story to the core. Morgan grew up in Titusville, in the shadow of the launch pads. The space program wasn't a distant national project to her. It was the industry of her hometown, unfolding right across the Indian River.

Because she was here, at the epicenter, when the work began, a local teenager could take a summer job and end up at a console for the moon landing. The place made it possible. Brevard County didn't just watch the space age happen. It raised some of the people who made it happen, and JoAnn Morgan is proof.

## What her story teaches us about our own

Here's what moves me most about JoAnn Morgan, and why I wanted to write about her.

For decades, hardly anyone knew her name. The famous photos of the firing room were right there, one woman among the men, and the story of who she was and what she did went largely untold until recent years. A person who helped launch humanity to the moon nearly became a footnote.

That's how it goes for the quiet ones who do extraordinary things. They don't tell their own stories, because they "just did their job." History overlooks them until someone thinks to ask. Your family has people like this too, the ones who did remarkable things without fanfare, whose stories will quietly disappear unless somebody sits them down and records them. JoAnn Morgan's life is a reminder that the most important story in the room is sometimes the one nobody's written down yet.

## Frequently asked questions

**Who was the only woman in the Apollo 11 firing room?**
JoAnn Morgan, an instrumentation controller for NASA, was the only woman in Kennedy Space Center's Launch Control Center firing room during the Apollo 11 liftoff on July 16, 1969. She grew up in Titusville, Florida, on the Space Coast.

**What did JoAnn Morgan do at NASA?**
Morgan was a measurement and instrumentation engineer who worked on the Mercury, Gemini, and Apollo programs. She served as instrumentation controller for Apollo 11 and later became the first woman to hold several senior leadership roles at Kennedy Space Center before retiring in 2003.

**Where did JoAnn Morgan grow up?**
Morgan moved to Titusville, Florida, as a high school junior and got her start with a summer trainee job at Cape Canaveral at age 17. Her Space Coast upbringing placed her at the center of the space program as it began.

**Why is JoAnn Morgan important?**
She broke barriers for women in engineering and spaceflight, was the only woman in the firing room for the historic Apollo 11 launch, and became the first woman in several senior NASA leadership positions. She was inducted into the Florida Women's Hall of Fame in 1995.
