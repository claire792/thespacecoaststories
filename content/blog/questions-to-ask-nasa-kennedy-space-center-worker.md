The best questions to ask someone who worked at NASA or Kennedy Space Center focus on their specific job, the missions they were part of, what launch days felt like from the inside, the people they worked with, and how it felt to be part of something historic. Let them tell the human story behind the hardware. Below are questions grouped by theme to help you preserve a space worker's memories, right here on the Space Coast.

Here on the Space Coast, some of the most extraordinary stories in America are sitting in ordinary living rooms. The neighbor who wired a spacecraft. The retiree who tracked a capsule from a console. The grandmother who packed parachutes or ran numbers that put people on the moon.

Most of them don't think of themselves as remarkable. They'll tell you they "just did their job." Your job is to ask the questions that reveal what an astonishing job it was, and to get it recorded before those firsthand memories are lost.

## Why these stories matter, and why now

The people who built and launched the early space program are aging, and much of what they carry has never been written down. NASA itself understands the value of these firsthand accounts, which is why it runs an [oral history project](https://www.nasa.gov/history/history-publications-and-resources/oral-histories/johnson-space-center/) preserving interviews with the people who did the work.

But NASA can't capture everyone, and it can't capture your family member the way you can, with the personal details, the home life, the whole person behind the badge. That part is up to us who live here.

## Questions about the work

1. How did you end up working at NASA or Kennedy Space Center?
2. What exactly was your job? Walk me through a normal day.
3. What did most people misunderstand about what you did?
4. What did your workspace, pad, or control room look and feel like?
5. What was the hardest technical problem you helped solve?
6. What tools or technology did you use that seem primitive now?
7. Who trained you, and what did you have to learn fast?

## Questions about the missions

8. Which missions were you part of?
9. What's the one you're proudest of?
10. Were you ever part of something that went wrong? What happened?
11. What was riding on your piece of the work?
12. Did you ever meet the astronauts? What were they like?
13. What's a detail about a famous mission that the public never knew?

## Questions about launch day

14. Where were you during a launch, and what was your role?
15. What did a launch feel like from the inside, the sound, the ground, the nerves?
16. What was going through your mind in the final countdown?
17. How did you celebrate when it went right?
18. What happened in the room when something went wrong?
19. Did it ever stop being amazing, or did it get routine?

## Questions about the people and the culture

20. Who did you work alongside, and what were they like?
21. What was the culture like, the pressure, the pride, the humor?
22. Did you feel like you were part of history while it happened, or only later?
23. What did your family think of your work?
24. Are there coworkers whose stories should also be preserved?

## Questions about the Space Coast then

25. What was this area like when you arrived?
26. How did the space program change our towns?
27. Where did everyone live, eat, and gather?
28. What was it like to raise a family in a place built around launches?
29. What's gone now from those days that you miss?

## Questions about looking back

30. How did this work shape your life?
31. What are you proudest of?
32. What do you want your grandchildren to understand about what you did?
33. What should history remember about the people behind the missions?
34. If you could say one thing to the young engineers working here now, what would it be?

## How to capture it well

Record it, always, with their permission. The technical details and the sequence of events are easy to lose, and their voice telling it is irreplaceable. Old photos, badges, mission patches, and press clippings are wonderful memory keys, so bring them out and ask what each one is.

For more technique, see our guides on [interviewing an elderly relative](/blog/how-to-interview-elderly-relative) and [recording a parent's life story](/blog/how-to-record-parents-life-story).

## Frequently asked questions

**What should I ask a retired NASA or Kennedy Space Center worker?**
Ask about their specific job and a typical day, the missions they worked on, what launch day felt like from the inside, the people and culture, how the space program changed the Space Coast, and how the work shaped their life. Let them tell the human story behind the technology.

**How do I preserve the story of a family member who worked in the space program?**
Interview them with open questions, record it with their permission, and use old photos, badges, and mission patches to jog memories. Capture both the technical details and the personal side. A personal historian can turn it into a lasting book, and NASA's oral history program shows how valued these firsthand accounts are.

**Why is it important to record Space Coast space program memories?**
The people who built and launched the early space program are aging, and much of their firsthand experience has never been written down. Once they're gone, those personal accounts, the human stories behind the missions, can't be recovered. Recording them now preserves a unique piece of local and national history.

**Where can I learn about NASA's own oral history efforts?**
NASA runs an oral history project that preserves interviews with people who worked in the space program. You can explore it through NASA's history resources online. It's a great example of why these firsthand stories are worth capturing, both nationally and within your own family.
