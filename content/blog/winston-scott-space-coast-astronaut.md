Winston Scott is a retired NASA astronaut and U.S. Navy captain who flew two Space Shuttle missions, walked in space three times, and has spent the last two decades making Florida's Space Coast his home. He wasn't born here. He came here the way a lot of us did, chose it, and never left.

What makes his story worth telling isn't just the two flights and the three spacewalks. It's how he got there. Winston Scott didn't grow up dreaming of rockets. He grew up playing music.

## The music major who ended up in space

Scott was born in Miami in 1950 and graduated from Coral Gables High School in 1968. When he went off to Florida State University, he wasn't chasing engineering or aviation. He earned a bachelor's degree in music. The word astronaut, by his own account, barely crossed his mind until well into college.

That's the part people miss about big lives. They rarely start on the runway you'd expect. Scott found his way to flight after graduation, entering Naval Aviation Officer Candidate School and earning his wings as a naval aviator in 1974. From there he flew some of the Navy's most demanding aircraft, the F-14 Tomcat and the F/A-18 Hornet among them, and logged more than 7,000 flight hours across two dozen types of aircraft, including over 200 landings on aircraft carriers. Somewhere in there he also earned a master's degree in aeronautical engineering.

A music degree, a fighter cockpit, an engineering graduate degree. It's a strange, wonderful path, and it's exactly the kind of life that gets flattened into one line in an obituary if nobody writes it down properly.

## Two shuttle missions and three walks in space

NASA selected Scott for its astronaut corps in 1992. He flew as a mission specialist on STS-72, launching aboard Space Shuttle Endeavour in January 1996, and again on STS-87 aboard Columbia in November 1997.

Between the two missions he spent more than 24 days in orbit and performed three spacewalks that added up to over 19 hours outside the vehicle. He carried music with him on those flights, fitting for a man who'd started his career with a trumpet instead of a joystick. If you've read our piece on [JoAnn Morgan, the only woman in the Apollo 11 firing room](/blog/joann-morgan-apollo-11-firing-room/), you already know the Space Coast is full of people whose real stories are far richer than the headline they're famous for. Scott is one of them.

## A Space Coast life after NASA

Scott retired from NASA and the Navy in 1999, but he didn't retire from the space program's home turf. He served as Executive Director of the Florida Space Authority, advising the state's leaders on space and aeronautics. Then he spent years at the Florida Institute of Technology in Melbourne, where he was Dean of the College of Aeronautics and later Professor Emeritus and a senior advisor to the university's president. He holds the rank of professor of aeronautics and professor of music, which tells you everything about how he sees the two halves of his life.

These days he serves as Director of Operational Excellence at the Kennedy Space Center Visitor Complex. He holds a second-degree black belt in Shotokan karate. And he still plays trumpet with bands up and down the Cape Canaveral coast. If you've ever caught live music somewhere in Brevard and didn't recognize the guy on trumpet, there's a small chance you were watching a man who's been to space twice.

## The story he chose to write down

In 2005, Scott published a memoir called *Reflections from Earth Orbit*. That title says something. He didn't just want to record what he did. He wanted to record what it meant, what it looked like to hang above the planet and see it whole.
