Remento, StoryWorth, and hiring a personal historian all help you capture a loved one's life story, but they suit very different families. StoryWorth emails weekly written questions and compiles the answers into a book. Remento sends prompts your relative answers out loud by video, then turns the recording into written stories. A personal historian is a real person who interviews your loved one, draws out the stories they'd never write down themselves, and shapes it all into a finished book. The right choice comes down to three things: how your relative likes to communicate, how much time you can put in, and how polished you want the result.

If you've been comparing tabs and getting nowhere, this guide lays out how each one actually works, who it's best for, and the honest tradeoffs, so you can pick with confidence.

## How StoryWorth works

StoryWorth is built around writing. Each week it emails your relative a question about their life, they write back, and after a year of prompts the answers get compiled and printed into a hardcover book.

It's a lovely model for the right person. If your mom or dad genuinely enjoys writing and will sit down most weeks to answer a thoughtful prompt, StoryWorth gives you a full year of their memories in their own words. The catch is the one every honest review mentions: the questions can pile up unanswered. Writing is work, and a busy or reluctant storyteller can drift off, leaving you with a half-finished book and a subscription you're not sure was worth it. It shines when the storyteller is self-motivated, or when a family member gently keeps them on pace. We go deeper on this in our post on [StoryWorth alternatives](/blog/storyworth-alternatives/).

**Best for:** relatives who like to write and will stick with a weekly habit.

## How Remento works

Remento was designed to fix StoryWorth's biggest weakness, the writing. Instead of typing, your relative gets a prompt and answers it out loud, usually by recording a short video on their phone. Remento then transcribes the spoken answer into a written story and keeps the original video, often linked from the finished book through a QR code you can scan to hear their voice.

For a lot of families, that's a meaningful upgrade. Plenty of people who freeze up at a blank page will happily talk for ten minutes, and talking captures personality, laughter, and the way someone actually tells a story. You also end up with their voice and face preserved, not just text. It's still a self-guided tool, though. Someone in the family has to set it up, keep the prompts moving, and see the project through to a finished book. The recording is easier than writing, but the follow-through is still on you.

**Best for:** relatives who'd rather talk than write, and families who want the voice and video saved.

## What a personal historian does

A personal historian isn't an app. It's a person. Someone who sits down with your loved one, interviews them across several conversations, and does the hardest part for you: drawing out the stories they'd never think to tell, let alone write down.

This is the difference people underestimate. The best family stories rarely come from a prompt. They come from a good listener asking the follow-up question, noticing the thread worth pulling, and giving someone permission to talk about the things that matter. A personal historian records those conversations, preserves the voice, then writes and shapes everything into a finished, professionally designed book, usually built around photographs. You don't manage the project. You hand it off and get an heirloom back. If you want to understand the role fully, start with [what a personal historian is and whether your family needs one](/blog/what-is-a-personal-historian/), and [how to choose a personal historian](/blog/how-to-choose-a-personal-historian/) when you're ready to compare them.

**Best for:** families who want the stories a relative won't tell on their own, and a keepsake-quality book without doing the work themselves.

## The honest tradeoff: cost, effort, and depth

Here's the plain version. These options trade off against each other on three axes, and no single choice wins all three.

StoryWorth and Remento are the low-cost options, typically a flat annual fee, but they put the effort and the follow-through on you and your relative. That's the hidden price. A subscription is cheap only if the book actually gets finished. A personal historian costs more, often into the thousands for a full book, but the effort drops to nearly zero on your end and the depth goes way up, because a trained interviewer gets stories out of people that no automated prompt ever will. For real numbers on the professional route, see [how much it costs to hire someone to write a family memoir](/blog/cost-to-hire-memoir-writer/).

Cheap and easy and deep. You get to pick two.

## So which should you choose?

Match the tool to your family, not the other way around.

If your relative loves to write and has the discipline for it, StoryWorth is a fine, affordable start. If they'd rather talk and you want their voice saved, Remento is the better self-guided pick. And if the person whose story you want is the type who says "oh, nobody wants to hear about my life," the one who'd never fill out a prompt but has more worth remembering than anyone in the family, that's exactly who a personal historian exists for. Time-short families, reluctant storytellers, and anyone who wants a true heirloom tend to land here.

There's also no rule that says you choose only one. Some families use an app to get the ball rolling, then bring in a personal historian to turn scattered answers into a real book.
