The best questions to ask your grandparents are the open ones, the kind that ask for a story instead of a yes or a no. Start with their childhood, then move to work, love, the hard years, and what they hope you'll remember. Below are fifty questions, grouped by theme, that you can bring to any visit and start using today.

Here's the thing nobody tells you. You don't run out of time all at once. It goes quietly. The stories are still in there, shelved and waiting, and then one day the person who could tell them can't anymore. Most families don't realize the door has closed until they're standing in front of it.

So this is a list you can actually use. Print it, screenshot it, tuck it in your bag before Sunday dinner. You don't have to get through all fifty. You barely have to get through five. The point isn't to interview anyone. The point is to ask one real question and then be quiet long enough to hear the answer.

A quick tip before the list: record it. Your phone's voice memo app is enough. Later on, the words on the page will matter, but the sound of their voice saying them will matter more.

## Questions about childhood and where they came from

1. Where were you born, and what was the house like?
2. What's your very first memory?
3. Who did you look up to when you were little, and why?
4. What did your family do for fun before there was money for much of anything?
5. What smell or sound takes you straight back to being a kid?
6. Were you a troublemaker or a rule-follower? Tell me the story that proves it.
7. What chores did you have, and which one did you hate most?
8. What did a normal dinner look like at your table growing up?

## Questions about growing up

9. What were you like as a teenager?
10. Who was your first crush, and whatever happened to them?
11. What did you want to be when you grew up? Did you get to be it?
12. What's something you did back then that your parents never found out about?
13. What song or movie meant everything to you at seventeen?
14. When did you first feel like an adult?
15. What's a fashion or fad you swore you'd never give up... and then did?

## Questions about love and marriage

16. How did you meet the person you married, or the great love of your life?
17. What do you remember about your first date?
18. When did you know?
19. What's the secret nobody tells you about staying together for decades?
20. What was your wedding day actually like, not the photos, the real day?
21. What's the hardest thing you two ever came through?
22. If you could go back to the beginning, what would you tell your younger self about love?

## Questions about family and raising kids

23. What were you most afraid of when you became a parent?
24. What's a moment as a parent you'd relive if you could?
25. What did your own parents get right that you tried to pass down?
26. What's a family tradition you hope we never let go of?
27. Is there a story about me, or my parent, that I've probably never heard?
28. What do you hope your grandchildren remember about you?

## Questions about work and getting by

29. What was your first job, and what did it pay?
30. What work are you proudest of?
31. Was there a job you took just to keep the lights on? Tell me about it.
32. Who taught you how to do the thing you got good at?
33. What did you learn about money the hard way?
34. If you'd had the freedom to do any work in the world, what would it have been?

## Questions about hard times and coming through them

35. What's the hardest year of your life, and how did you get to the other side of it?
36. Who did you lose too soon?
37. What did you worry about back then that turned out fine?
38. When were you the most scared, and what did you do?
39. What's something you survived that you rarely talk about?
40. Where did you find your strength when you didn't think you had any left?

## Questions about home, place, and history

41. How has this town changed since you got here?
42. What did the world look like out your window when you were young?
43. What historical moment do you remember living through?
44. What did people get wrong about "the good old days"?
45. What's gone now that you wish had stayed?

## The ones people forget to ask

46. What are you proudest of, all told?
47. Is there anything you've never told anyone that you'd want us to know?
48. What do you know now that you wish you'd known at twenty-five?
49. When were you happiest?
50. What do you hope we do with all of this after you're gone?

## How to actually use this list

Don't sit anyone down under a bright light with a clipboard. That's a fast way to get short answers.

Pick a comfortable afternoon. Ask one question and then wait. Let the silence sit, even when it stretches. The good stuff usually comes after the pause, once they've decided you really want to hear it. Follow the thread they seem to want to pull, not the next number on the page.

And keep the recorder running the whole time, even through the tangents. The tangent is often the real story.

## Frequently asked questions

**What are good questions to ask grandparents?**
Open-ended ones that invite a story instead of a single fact. Ask about their childhood home, how they met their spouse, the hardest year they lived through, and what they hope you'll remember. The fifty questions above are grouped by theme so you can start anywhere.

**How do I record my grandparents' stories?**
Use your phone's voice memo app, sit somewhere quiet and comfortable, and ask one open question at a time. Record the whole conversation, back it up to the cloud or email it to yourself, and don't worry about editing in the moment. Capturing it is what counts.

**What if my grandparent doesn't want to talk about the past?**
Start small and low-stakes. Ask about a favorite meal, a first job, or a song they loved. Warmth opens doors that direct questions don't. If it's still hard, a neutral third person asking the questions often gets stories a family member never could.
