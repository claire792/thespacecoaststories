A legacy letter, also called an ethical will, is a personal message that passes on your values, life lessons, blessings, and hopes to the people you love. Unlike a legal will, which distributes your money and property, a legacy letter is not legally binding. It shares who you are and what you believe. Here's what it is, why it belongs alongside your estate planning, and how to write one.

You spend real effort deciding who gets the house and the savings. But here's a question worth sitting with: when you're gone, what will your family wish they had in your own words? Almost never is it the account numbers. It's you, what you believed, what you learned, what you wanted them to carry.

That's what a legacy letter captures. Let me explain.

## What is a legacy letter or ethical will?

An ethical will is an old tradition, older than the legal kind, of passing down not your possessions but your values and wisdom. Today it's often called a legacy letter, and it's simply a heartfelt message to your loved ones sharing who you are and what matters to you.

It isn't a legal document, and it doesn't distribute anything. It can be a letter, a recording, or part of a larger life-story book. Its whole purpose is to pass on the things a legal will can't hold.

## How it's different from a legal will

These two do completely different jobs, and families benefit from both.

| | Legal will | Legacy letter / ethical will |
|---|---|---|
| **Passes on** | Money, property, assets | Values, lessons, blessings, stories |
| **Legally binding?** | Yes | No |
| **Written with** | An attorney | Yourself (or a personal historian) |
| **Answers** | "Who gets what?" | "Who was I, and what do I hope for you?" |

One handles your estate. The other handles your heart. Neither replaces the other.

## Why it belongs with your estate planning

Estate attorneys increasingly encourage clients to write a legacy letter alongside their legal documents, and for good reason. The legal will prevents conflict over things. The legacy letter gives the family something far harder to put a price on: comfort, meaning, and a sense of the person's blessing after they're gone.

It can also ease the legal side. When heirs understand the *why* behind decisions, in a parent's own warm words, there's often less hurt and less fighting over the *what*. Just remember the key distinction: a legacy letter is not legally binding, so it should never be used to give instructions about assets. Keep the wishes about property in your legal will, and keep the values and love in your legacy letter.

## What to include in a legacy letter

There's no formula, but these prompts help:

1. **Values.** What principles guided your life? What do you hope your family lives by?
2. **Lessons.** What did life teach you, sometimes the hard way?
3. **Gratitude.** Who and what are you thankful for?
4. **Stories.** A few defining moments that made you who you are.
5. **Blessings and hopes.** What do you wish for each of your loved ones?
6. **Forgiveness and peace.** Anything you want to heal, release, or make sure is said.

## How to write one

Start small. You don't have to write it all at once, and it doesn't have to be polished.

Write the way you talk, as if you're speaking directly to your children or grandchildren. Be specific and honest. You can address it to the whole family or write separate notes to individuals. Date it, sign it, and tell someone where to find it. Many people revisit and add to theirs over the years, which is a beautiful thing to do.

## When to write it

The honest answer is now. People tend to put this off for a "someday" that isn't guaranteed, and a legacy letter only works if it gets written. If you're already doing estate planning, it's the perfect moment to write the other half. And it's never too early. You can always add to it as life goes on.

## Frequently asked questions

**What is an ethical will or legacy letter?**
It's a personal message passing on your values, life lessons, gratitude, and hopes to your loved ones. It's an old tradition, distinct from a legal will, and it shares who you are rather than distributing your assets. It can be written or recorded.

**Is a legacy letter legally binding?**
No. A legacy letter or ethical will is not a legal document and carries no legal authority. It complements your legal will but should never be used to give instructions about money or property. Keep asset decisions in your legally binding will.

**How is a legacy letter different from a will?**
A legal will distributes your money and property and is legally binding. A legacy letter passes on your values, wisdom, and blessings and is not legally binding. One answers "who gets what," the other answers "who was I and what do I hope for you." Families benefit from having both.

**How do I write an ethical will?**
Write as if speaking directly to your loved ones. Include your values, lessons learned, gratitude, a few defining stories, and your hopes and blessings for each person. Keep it honest and personal, date and sign it, and let someone know where to find it. You can add to it over time.
