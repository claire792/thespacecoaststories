To protect your family photos and important documents during hurricane season, digitize them first so a copy survives no matter what happens to the originals, store the physical items in a waterproof and fireproof container kept up high and away from windows, and keep at least one backup somewhere off-site or in the cloud. On the Space Coast, the time to do this is before a storm is ever named, not when one's already sitting in the forecast cone.

We prepare our homes for hurricane season every year. We buy the water, check the shutters, fill the gas cans. But the one thing you truly can't replace, the box of photos, the letters, your grandmother's handwriting, is usually the thing nobody makes a plan for. Let's fix that.

## Why hurricane season is a quiet threat to family history

Here in Brevard County, we know the drill. Wind gets the headlines, but water does the damage. A roof leak, a foot of flooding, a few days without power in July humidity, any one of those can destroy a lifetime of photos and documents faster than you'd think.

Paper and old photographs are fragile in exactly the ways a storm is dangerous. They absorb water. They grow mold within a day or two in the heat. Ink runs, prints stick together, and the emulsion on old photos lifts right off. And unlike your patio furniture, none of it is replaceable. That's what makes family history worth a real plan, not an afterthought.

## Digitize first: the single most important step

If you do only one thing this season, do this. Scan or photograph your irreplaceable photos and documents, because a digital copy is the one version a hurricane can't touch.

You don't need fancy gear. A flatbed scanner is ideal, but a phone camera in good light does a fine job for most photos and papers. Work through the true irreplaceables first: old family photos, letters, certificates, anything one-of-a-kind. Then follow the simple rule the pros use, keep more than one copy, in more than one place. A copy on your computer plus a copy in the cloud means a flooded house and a dead hard drive still can't erase your family's memory. ## Protect the physical originals

Digitizing saves the image. You still want to protect the actual objects, because the original photo in your mother's hand is its own kind of treasure.

Move your most precious originals into a waterproof, fireproof container or safe. Keep that container up high, on a shelf or a closet's top level, never on the floor of a room that could flood. Keep it away from windows and exterior walls where wind-driven rain gets in first. And whatever you do, don't store your family archive in the garage or an outdoor shed, which are the first places to flood and the worst for heat and humidity year-round. Archival sleeves and acid-free boxes add another layer of protection and slow down the everyday damage that happens even without a storm.

## Make a grab-and-go memory plan

When an evacuation order comes, you'll have minutes, not hours, and you'll be thinking about people and pets first. Decide now what you'd grab.

Pick out the handful of truly irreplaceable items and keep them together in one labeled, portable box or bag you can carry to the car in a single trip. Make a short list of what matters most and where it lives, and share it with your family. If everything important is already scanned and backed up to the cloud, this step gets a lot lighter, because the memories are safe even if the box stays behind.

## After the storm: how to save water-damaged photos

If photos do get wet, don't panic and don't wait. Quick, gentle action saves more than you'd expect.

Rinse off mud or dirty water with clean, cool water. Lay the photos face up on a flat surface to air dry, out of direct sunlight, and never stack wet photos or they'll fuse into a solid brick. If you're overwhelmed by the volume, and after a bad storm you might be, seal the wet photos in plastic bags and put them in the freezer. Freezing halts the damage and buys you time to dry them in batches later. It sounds strange. It works.
