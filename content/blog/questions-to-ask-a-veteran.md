The best questions to ask a veteran are open ones that let them share at their own pace, covering why they joined, their training, the people they served with, daily life, deployment, coming home, and what it all taught them. Let them lead, don't push on painful subjects, and record it if they're willing. Below are questions grouped by theme to help you preserve a veteran's story with respect.

A veteran's service is often the most defining chapter of their life, and one of the least talked about at the dinner table. Many veterans have carried their stories quietly for decades, waiting, whether they'd say so or not, for someone to ask with genuine care.

These questions are for asking with that care. A word before you start: let the veteran set the pace. Some memories are a source of pride, and some are wounds. If they steer away from something, let them. Trust keeps the door open.

## Before you begin: a few ground rules

- **Let them lead.** Follow the stories they want to tell before the ones you're curious about.
- **Never push on trauma.** If a question lands hard, ease off. A gentle "we can skip that" honors them.
- **Record it if they agree.** A veteran's own voice telling these stories is a treasure for the whole family. Ask first.
- **Thank them, and mean it.** Being asked, and truly listened to, is often deeply moving.

## Questions about joining

1. What was going on in your life when you enlisted or were drafted?
2. Why did you choose your branch of service?
3. How did your family feel about you going?
4. How old were you, and how ready did you feel?
5. Do you remember the day you left for basic training?

## Questions about training

6. Where did you go for basic training, and what was it like?
7. What was the hardest part of training?
8. Who was your drill instructor, and what do you remember about them?
9. What did the military teach you that stuck with you for life?
10. Was there a moment you thought you wouldn't make it through?

## Questions about the people

11. Who were your closest friends in the service?
12. Is there someone you served with you never forgot?
13. What did you do for fun or comfort when you were off duty?
14. Are there people you're still in touch with, or wish you were?
15. Did anyone look out for you, or did you look out for someone?

## Questions about daily life

16. What was an ordinary day actually like?
17. What did you eat, and where did you sleep?
18. What did you carry with you that mattered?
19. What did you miss most from home?
20. What sights, sounds, or smells still take you back?

## Questions about deployment and service

21. Where were you stationed or deployed?
22. What was your job, and what did it involve day to day?
23. What are you proudest of from your service?
24. Was there a moment that changed how you saw things?
25. What do you wish people understood about what it was really like?

*(Follow their lead here especially. Let them share only what they choose.)*

## Questions about coming home

26. What was the day you came home like?
27. How did it feel to be back? Was it what you expected?
28. What was hardest about returning to civilian life?
29. Did people understand what you'd been through?
30. What helped you find your footing again?

## Questions about looking back

31. How did your service shape the rest of your life?
32. What did you learn about yourself?
33. Is there anything you'd want younger service members to know?
34. What do you want your family and grandchildren to remember about this?
35. If you could say one thing to the people you served with, what would it be?

## A meaningful next step: donate the story to history

Here's something many families don't know. The Library of Congress runs the **Veterans History Project**, which collects and preserves firsthand accounts of American war veterans so future generations can hear them. If your veteran is willing, you can record an interview and donate it to the national archive, making their voice part of the country's permanent history.

You can learn how at the [Veterans History Project's official page](https://www.loc.gov/programs/veterans-history-project/how-to-participate/). It's a beautiful way to honor their service beyond your own family.

## Frequently asked questions

**What questions should I ask a veteran about their service?**
Ask open-ended questions grouped by theme: why they joined, their training, the people they served with, daily life, their deployment or job, coming home, and what it taught them. Let the veteran lead and go only where they're comfortable. The themed questions above are a respectful place to start.

**How do I talk to a veteran about difficult memories?**
Let them set the pace and never push. If a question brings up something painful, ease off with a simple "we can skip that." Focus on the stories they want to tell, listen without judgment, and let trust build. Comfortable topics often open the door to deeper ones over time.

**Should I record a veteran's story?**
Yes, if they agree. Their own voice telling these stories is priceless for the family. Ask permission first, use a phone or recorder, and keep it low-key. You can also donate the recording to the Library of Congress Veterans History Project to preserve it in the national archive.

**When is a good time to ask a veteran about their service?**
Any unhurried, private moment works, and occasions like Veterans Day or Memorial Day can open the conversation naturally. The most important thing is starting while they're still here to tell it, at a pace that feels right to them.
