A dementia or Alzheimer's diagnosis doesn't mean your loved one's stories are already gone. Long-term memories, the childhood ones, the deep ones, often stay reachable long after recent memory fades. To preserve them, start recording sooner rather than later, meet the person where they are, and use photos, music, and old objects to open the door. Here's how, gently.

If you've just gotten this news about someone you love, take a breath. I know the fear underneath the question. You're afraid that the person and their whole history are slipping away, and that one day soon you won't be able to reach them at all.

Here's the part people don't tell you early enough: there's still time, and there's still a lot in there. But it's the kind of time you don't want to waste, because the window narrows. Let's use it well.

## What dementia does to memory, and what it spares

Dementia usually takes recent memory first. Your loved one may not remember lunch, or your last visit, while remembering the summer of 1958 in vivid detail. That's not random. Older, deeper memories are stored differently, and they often hold on far longer.

That's the opening. The stories you most want, the childhood, the young love, the early years, are frequently the very ones still within reach, even well into the disease. So the goal shifts. You're not fighting to hold onto everything. You're gathering the deep stories while they're still there to gather.

## Start sooner than feels necessary

The single most important thing I can tell you: don't wait for a better time.

Every stage that passes tends to take a little more. The person who can walk you through their wedding day today may only have fragments of it in six months. Capturing their stories early, while the disease is mild, protects the richest version. If you're deciding whether it's too soon, it almost certainly isn't.

## How to connect and draw out memories

1. **Meet them in their reality, don't correct it.** If your mother thinks it's 1970, or asks for someone long gone, don't argue or quiz her. Correcting causes distress and closes the door. Step into her world and follow it. You'll both get more out of the visit.

2. **Use photographs as keys.** An old picture unlocks memory in a way a direct question can't. Hand them a photo and ask who's in it and what was happening. Even when names slip, the feelings and stories often come.

3. **Let music do its work.** Music reaches people with dementia when almost nothing else can. A song from their youth can bring back a whole era, and sometimes the words to go with it. Play what they loved at twenty.

4. **Engage the senses.** A familiar smell, a recipe, a texture, a hymn. Sensory cues reach memories that words can't get to. Bake the thing their mother used to bake and see what surfaces.

5. **Time your visits well.** Many people with dementia are clearer earlier in the day and more confused or agitated by late afternoon and evening, a pattern often called sundowning. Do your remembering in the morning when you can.

6. **Ask about feelings, not just facts.** Even after the details go, the emotional truth stays. "Were you happy then?" often lands when "what year was that?" doesn't. Follow the feeling.

7. **Record whatever you can, however you can.** Voice memos, short videos, notes right after a visit. Don't wait for a perfect setup. A phone in your pocket is enough, and the sound of their voice will matter more than you can imagine later.

## Keep the pressure off

This isn't a race to extract data, and treating it that way backfires. Some visits, the stories flow. Some visits, they don't, and the win is simply a good hour together. Both are worth it.

Meet your loved one with patience and no agenda beyond connection, and you'll be surprised what comes on its own. The goal is closeness. The stories tend to follow closeness, not pressure.

This is heavy work, and you're often doing it while grieving someone who's still here. That's one of the hardest things there is. You don't have to carry all of it.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can you still capture the memories of someone with dementia?**
Often, yes, especially early. Dementia usually affects recent memory first while sparing older, deeper memories, so childhood and early-life stories frequently stay accessible well into the disease. The key is to start capturing them sooner rather than later.

**What's the best way to talk to a loved one with Alzheimer's about the past?**
Meet them in their reality instead of correcting them, use photos, music, and familiar smells to open memories, ask about feelings rather than exact facts, and visit earlier in the day when they're often clearer. Keep it warm and pressure-free.

**Should I record my parent's stories after a dementia diagnosis?**
Yes, and soon. Each stage of the disease tends to take a little more, so capturing stories while symptoms are mild protects the fullest version. Even simple phone voice memos or short videos are enough to preserve their voice and memories.

**Why does music help people with dementia remember?**
Musical memory is stored in parts of the brain that dementia tends to affect later, so familiar songs can reach a person and bring back associated memories and emotions even when other approaches don't. Playing music from their youth is a gentle, effective way to connect.
