To preserve your family recipes, gather them from every source you can find, record the cooks who never wrote anything down, and save both the recipe and its story in a form that lasts, like a printed family cookbook or a backed-up digital archive. The recipe is only half of it. The other half is who made it, why it mattered, and the sound of them telling you.

A recipe card is one of the most honest documents a family owns. The handwriting, the splatter marks, the little note that says "Grandma's, do not double the salt." Lose that, and you don't just lose a dish. You lose a small piece of a person.

## Start with the recipes nobody ever wrote down

The most important recipes in your family are usually the ones that don't exist on paper. They live in someone's hands. A grandmother who cooks by feel, a dad who's made the same sauce every Sunday for forty years and has never once measured it.

Those are the ones to capture first, because they leave with the cook. Don't ask them to write it down. They can't, not really, because they don't think in cups and teaspoons. Instead, cook it with them. Stand in the kitchen and watch what they actually do. When they toss in "a little" flour, stop and measure it into a cup so you can write down what "a little" means. Record the whole thing on your phone, video if they'll let you, audio if they're shy. You'll capture the technique, the timing, and their voice walking you through it. That recording is worth more than the recipe.

This is the same approach that works for any life story. If you've read our guide on [how to interview an elderly relative without it feeling like an interview](/blog/how-to-interview-elderly-relative/), you already know the trick: don't sit them down and grill them. Do something together and let the stories come out sideways. A kitchen is perfect for that.

## Gather the recipes that already exist

Once you've captured what's only in someone's head, collect what's already on paper. Pull together the recipe cards, the stained pages in the church cookbook, the backs of envelopes, the recipes clipped from newspapers decades ago and taped into a binder.

Photograph or scan each one exactly as it is. Don't retype and toss the original. The handwriting is the point. So are the notes in the margin and the grease stains that prove the recipe got used. Save those images in at least two places, a computer and a cloud backup or an external drive, so a single spill or a dead hard drive can't erase them. For more on this, our post on [how to organize old family photos](/blog/how-to-organize-old-family-photos/) walks through the same digitize-and-back-up habit, and it applies just as well to recipes.

## Capture the story, not just the ingredients

Here's what separates a recipe collection from a family cookbook: the stories. A list of ingredients is a set of instructions. A recipe with a story attached is a memory you can taste.

So for each dish, write down a few sentences. Who made it. Where it came from. When it showed up, the holiday, the funeral, the ordinary Tuesday. Why it mattered. The pierogi that took three generations and a whole day to make. The pound cake that appeared at every single birthday. The chili that started an argument every Thanksgiving about whether beans belong in it.

These little notes are the difference between a binder your kids ignore and a book they fight over.

## Turn it into something that lasts

Once you've gathered the recipes and the stories, put them somewhere your family will actually keep them.

A printed family cookbook is the classic choice, and for good reason. You can organize it by person, by course, or by occasion, drop in photos of the cooks and the food, and print enough copies for everyone. It's a gift that gets used, not shelved. A digital archive works too, especially a shared folder the whole family can add to over time. Whatever you choose, make sure there's more than one copy, and make sure someone knows where it lives.
