The best StoryWorth alternative depends on how your loved one likes to communicate. StoryWorth emails weekly questions that your parent answers in writing, then compiles a year of answers into a book. If your parent doesn't like to write, better options include recording their answers as audio, using a question-card kit, or hiring a personal historian who interviews them and writes the book for them. Here's an honest comparison.

StoryWorth is popular for a good reason. It's a lovely, affordable idea: a weekly emailed question, a written answer, and a printed book at the end of the year. For a parent who enjoys writing and sits down consistently, it works beautifully.

But it isn't the right fit for everyone, and if you're here, you probably already sense that. Let me lay out the real alternatives, honestly, and who each one actually suits.

## How StoryWorth works (so we're comparing fairly)

StoryWorth sends your family member one question by email each week for a year. They reply in writing, the answers collect over the months, and at the end you can order a printed book of them. It runs on a yearly subscription, with printed books priced separately. You can see the current details on the [official StoryWorth site](https://welcome.storyworth.com/).

The model has two quiet assumptions: that the person likes writing, and that they'll keep up with it week after week. When both are true, it's great. When either isn't, the book stalls.

## Who StoryWorth isn't ideal for

Be honest about your own parent. StoryWorth may frustrate them if:

- They don't enjoy writing, or find it tiring
- Their eyesight, arthritis, or memory makes typing hard
- They're more of a talker than a writer
- They won't keep up a weekly habit for a year
- Their memory is fading and needs gentle, in-person drawing out

None of that means the stories can't be captured. It means writing-by-email is the wrong tool. Here's what fits better.

## The alternatives, and who each is best for

| Option | How it works | Best for | Roughly what it costs |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Record it yourself** | You interview your parent and record audio or video | Families with time, and a parent who'd rather talk than write | Free to low (your time + a phone) |
| **Question-card kit** | A deck of prompts you work through together | A warm, in-person activity; getting started cheaply | Low |
| **AI-assisted apps** | App prompts and transcribes spoken answers | Tech-comfortable families wanting spoken, not written | Low to moderate subscription |
| **Personal historian** | A pro interviews your parent and writes the book | A parent who won't write; wanting it done well and to keep | Higher (professional service) |

### If your parent would rather talk than write

This is the most common reason people look past StoryWorth, and the fix is simple: let them talk. Record the conversations instead of asking them to type. You can do this yourself with our guide to [recording a parent's life story](/blog/how-to-record-parents-life-story), or with a card kit to keep it flowing.

### If you want it done for you, and done to last

A personal historian is the full-service alternative. Instead of homework for your parent, it's a series of warm interviews, and the writing, design, and printing are handled for you. It costs more than a subscription because a person is doing the interviewing and the writing, but you end up with a real, polished book in your parent's voice, with no weekly nagging and nothing left half-finished. That's the work I do; here's [how a legacy book comes together](/blog/how-a-legacy-book-gets-made).

## How to choose

Match the tool to the person, not the other way around.

If your parent writes happily and reliably, StoryWorth is a fine, budget-friendly choice. If they'd rather talk, record them. If you want a shared activity, get the cards. If you want it truly handled and made to keep, hire a personal historian. The goal is the same in every case: the stories, saved. Pick whichever path actually gets you there for your family.

## Frequently asked questions

**What is the best alternative to StoryWorth if my parent doesn't like writing?**
Capture their stories as spoken audio or video instead of written answers. You can interview and record them yourself, use a question-card kit to guide the conversation, or hire a personal historian who interviews them and writes the book for them. All three suit a parent who'd rather talk than type.

**Is a personal historian better than StoryWorth?**
It's not better or worse, it's different. StoryWorth is an affordable, do-it-yourself subscription that relies on your parent writing weekly. A personal historian is a full-service option that interviews your parent and writes the book for them, which is worth it if your parent won't write or you want a polished keepsake without the weekly effort.

**Are there free alternatives to StoryWorth?**
Yes. Interviewing your parent yourself and recording it on a phone costs nothing but time, and it captures their actual voice. You can pair it with a free list of questions to guide the conversation and organize the results into a book later.

**What if my parent's memory is fading?**
Skip the written, weekly-homework model and capture stories in person, gently and soon. Recorded conversations, familiar photos, and an unhurried pace work far better than emailed questions. A personal historian experienced with memory loss can help draw out the stories while they're still reachable.
