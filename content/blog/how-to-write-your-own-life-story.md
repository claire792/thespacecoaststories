*Published July 13, 2026*

To write your own life story, you don't need to be a writer. Start by breaking your life into chapters or themes, write about one memory at a time in your own natural voice, use prompts to jog your memory, and don't worry about polish on the first pass. Aim for a little at a time, and let the stories add up. Here's a simple way to actually get it done.

Maybe you've thought about writing down your life for your kids or grandkids, and then thought, "but I'm not a writer." Set that worry down. Some of the most moving life stories I've ever read came from people who'd never written a thing.

You don't need to be a writer. You need to be honest, and you need a way to start. Here's both.

## First, let go of "writing a book"

The word "book" scares people into never beginning. So don't write a book. Write one memory. Then another.

A life story is just a pile of true memories, told plainly and put in order. You don't have to write it start to finish, and you don't have to make it beautiful. You have to get it down. Everything else can come later.

## A simple structure

Break your life into chunks so it's not one giant blank page. Most people find it easiest to go by era:

1. **Roots** — where you came from, your parents, your earliest world
2. **Childhood** — home, school, the shape of your young life
3. **Coming of age** — first jobs, first loves, becoming yourself
4. **Building a life** — marriage, family, work, the main years
5. **Trials** — the hard times and how you came through
6. **Reflections** — what you learned, what you believe, what you hope

Take them one at a time. Finishing one short section feels good and pulls you into the next.

## Prompts to get you unstuck

When you don't know what to write, answer a question instead. Just talk onto the page:

- What's your earliest memory?
- What was your childhood home like?
- How did you meet your spouse?
- What's the hardest thing you ever came through?
- What are you proudest of?
- What do you want your grandchildren to know?

For a much longer list, our [100 life story questions](/blog/100-life-story-interview-questions) will keep you writing for months.

## Tips to actually finish

- **Write like you talk.** Don't reach for fancy words. Your natural voice is exactly what your family wants to hear.
- **A little at a time.** Fifteen minutes, one memory. Consistency finishes books; marathons don't.
- **Don't edit while you write.** Get it down first. Fix it later, or don't.
- **Tell the truth.** The real, human moments matter far more than a shiny version. Include the struggles.
- **Talk it if you can't type it.** Record yourself telling a story out loud, then transcribe it. Many people find speaking easier than writing.

## When you'd rather have help

Some people love this process. Others start, stall, and realize they'd rather talk while someone else does the writing. Both are fine. If sitting down to write it yourself isn't happening, that's exactly what a personal historian is for: you talk, I write, and you still end up with your story in your own voice. Either way, please don't let "I'm not a writer" stop you. Your story is worth telling, however it gets told.

## Frequently asked questions

**How do I start writing my life story?**
Start small. Break your life into eras or themes, then write about one memory at a time in your natural voice, using prompts to jog your memory. Don't try to write a whole book at once, and don't polish as you go. A little at a time, and the stories add up.

**Do I need to be a good writer to write my memoir?**
No. Some of the most powerful life stories come from people who never considered themselves writers. Write the way you talk, focus on telling the truth of your experiences, and don't worry about fancy language. Your natural voice is what your family wants.

**How long should my life story be?**
As long or short as you like. It can be a few key stories or a full account of your life. What matters is capturing the memories that mean the most, not hitting a page count. Even a short, honest life story is a treasure to your family.

**What if I can't finish writing it myself?**
That's common. You can record yourself telling stories out loud and transcribe them, or work with a personal historian who interviews you and does the writing, so you still end up with your story in your own voice without having to write it yourself.
