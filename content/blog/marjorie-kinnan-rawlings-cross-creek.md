*Published June 29, 2026 · Updated June 29, 2026*

Marjorie Kinnan Rawlings was the Pulitzer Prize-winning author of The Yearling, who moved to the tiny settlement of Cross Creek, Florida, in 1928 and built her life's work around her rural neighbors' voices. She filled notebooks with their dialect, recipes, and stories, much the way a personal historian does. Here's her Florida story.

Marjorie Kinnan Rawlings won the Pulitzer Prize for a novel about a Florida boy and his fawn. But the deeper story is how she got it: by moving to a remote Florida crossroads, befriending her neighbors, and carefully preserving the way they talked, cooked, and lived. She's another kindred spirit to the work I do.

## The short version

- **August 8, 1896** — Born in Washington, D.C.
- **Childhood** — Shows literary talent early, publishing stories as a girl
- **1928** — Moves to a 72-acre orange grove at Cross Creek, in rural north-central Florida
- **1930s** — Immerses herself in her neighbors' lives, filling notebooks with their dialect and customs
- **1938** — Publishes *The Yearling*, her most celebrated novel
- **1939** — Wins the Pulitzer Prize for Fiction
- **1942** — Publishes *Cross Creek*, a memoir of her Florida neighbors and life
- **December 14, 1953** — Dies in St. Augustine, Florida

## Three turning points

**Moving to Cross Creek.** In 1928, Rawlings used an inheritance to buy an orange grove in the tiny Florida settlement of Cross Creek. It transformed her writing. The remote, rural place and its people gave her the subject she'd been missing, and she never looked back.

**Learning to truly listen.** Rawlings didn't write about her "cracker" neighbors from a distance. She spent real time with families like the Longs, visiting, listening, and filling notebooks with regional dialect, animal lore, and local recipes. She did the patient fieldwork of a listener, capturing a way of life exactly as it was lived and spoken.

**The Yearling and the Pulitzer.** All that listening became *The Yearling* in 1938, which won the Pulitzer Prize the next year and was made into a beloved film. She'd turned the overlooked lives of rural Florida into literature the whole country embraced.

## How Florida shaped her

Rawlings became the writer she was because of Florida. Cross Creek made her. Her greatest work is soaked in the landscape, the speech, and the people of rural north-central Florida, a world she chose, joined, and loved. Without that move to the Creek, there's no Yearling and no Pulitzer.

## What her story teaches us about our own

Here's why I wanted her in this series.

Marjorie Kinnan Rawlings understood something essential: that the lives of "ordinary" rural people, their dialect, their recipes, their daily struggles, were worthy of being written down and remembered. She took the voices that the wider world overlooked and made sure they lasted. That's the same conviction behind every family story worth preserving.

She also reminds us how fast a way of life can vanish. The world she captured at Cross Creek, the speech, the customs, the rhythms, was already disappearing, and much of what we know of it survives because she wrote it down. Your family holds a version of this too: ways of living, sayings, and recipes from a time that's slipping away. Someone has to be the one who writes it down before it's gone. Rawlings chose to be that person for rural Florida. You can be that person for your family.

## Frequently asked questions

**What is Marjorie Kinnan Rawlings famous for?**
She is best known for her 1938 novel *The Yearling*, which won the 1939 Pulitzer Prize for Fiction, and for *Cross Creek* (1942), a memoir of her life among her rural Florida neighbors.

**Where did Marjorie Kinnan Rawlings live in Florida?**
Rawlings lived at Cross Creek, a small settlement in rural north-central Florida near Hawthorne, where she bought an orange grove in 1928. The place and its people inspired most of her major work.

**How did Rawlings research her writing?**
She immersed herself in her neighbors' lives, spending time with local families and filling notebooks with their dialect, customs, animal lore, and recipes. That close listening gave her work its authenticity, much like the fieldwork of a personal historian or folklorist.
