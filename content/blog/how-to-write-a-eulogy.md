To write a eulogy that sounds like the person, gather specific stories and their own sayings, choose two or three qualities that captured who they were, and tell short true stories that show those qualities instead of listing facts. Aim for about three to five minutes, write the way you talk, and read it aloud before the day. Here's how, step by step.

Being asked to give a eulogy is an honor and a weight at the same time. You want to get it right, for them and for everyone in the room, and you're trying to do it while your heart is broken.

Take a breath. You don't need to be a writer. You need to help everyone remember the person you all loved. Here's how to do that, gently and well.

## What is a eulogy, and how long should it be?

A eulogy is a short speech that honors someone who has died, usually given at their funeral or celebration of life. It's not a biography and not a résumé. It's a portrait, painted in a few stories, of who this person actually was.

Keep it to about three to five minutes, which is roughly 500 to 750 words spoken. That's long enough to say something real and short enough to hold a grieving room. When in doubt, shorter and heartfelt beats long and complete.

## Step 1: Gather stories before you write

Don't start with a blank page. Start by collecting.

Call a few family members and friends and ask each one: what's your favorite memory of them? What did they always say? What were they like when no one was watching? You'll hear the same qualities come up again and again, and those repeated notes are the heart of your eulogy.

Our [fifty questions to ask](/blog/questions-to-ask-your-grandparents) can help jog memories if you're gathering from family.

## Step 2: Choose two or three qualities

You can't capture a whole person in five minutes, so don't try. Pick two or three traits that were most *them*. Her stubborn generosity. His terrible jokes and enormous heart. The way she made every stranger a friend.

Those qualities become your structure. Everything else falls away.

## Step 3: Use a simple structure

Here's a template that works every time. Fill it in and you have a eulogy.

| Part | What goes here | Roughly |
|---|---|---|
| **Opening** | Who you are and your relationship to them | 2–3 sentences |
| **The heart** | Your 2–3 chosen qualities, each shown through one short story | The bulk of it |
| **Their own words** | A saying, a phrase, a bit of their humor or wisdom | A few lines |
| **Closing** | What they gave you, and a simple goodbye | 3–4 sentences |

## Step 4: Show, don't list

This is the difference between a eulogy that sounds like them and one that could be about anyone.

Don't say "she was generous." Tell the thirty-second story about her pressing a twenty into your hand every time you left, no matter how little she had. The story proves the quality, and it brings her into the room in a way an adjective never can. One specific memory is worth a page of praise.

## Step 5: Write the way you talk

Read a draft out loud. If it sounds like a formal essay, loosen it. Use contractions. Use their real nickname. Let a little humor in, because the people who loved them will be grateful for permission to smile.

The best eulogies sound like a person talking about someone they loved, not a speech being performed.

## Delivering it on the day

A few things that help when the moment comes:

- **Print it large**, double-spaced, on paper you can hold. Don't rely on your phone.
- **It's okay to cry.** Pause, breathe, take a sip of water. The room is with you, not judging you.
- **Ask a backup.** Have someone ready to step in and finish reading if you can't. Knowing they're there often means you won't need them.
- **Slow down.** Grief speeds us up. Read slower than feels natural.

## What to avoid

Don't try to tell the person's whole life in order. Don't air old grievances or inside jokes only three people understand. Don't read a long list of accomplishments. And don't pad it with generic lines about heaven or clichés about angels unless they're genuinely yours. Specific and true always beats grand and hollow.

## If you're struggling to find the words

Some losses are too fresh, or the relationship was too complicated, to write about easily. That's okay. Lean on the stories other people give you. And know that the fact that you're trying, that you cared enough to labor over the right words, is itself a form of love everyone will feel.

## Frequently asked questions

**How long should a eulogy be?**
About three to five minutes, or roughly 500 to 750 words spoken. That's long enough to say something meaningful and short enough to hold a grieving audience. If you're unsure, err on the shorter side and make every line count.

**How do I write a eulogy that sounds like the person?**
Gather specific stories and their real sayings, choose two or three qualities that defined them, and show each quality through a short true story rather than listing facts. Write the way you talk, include their own words and humor, and read it aloud before the day.

**What should a eulogy include?**
An opening that says who you are, a heart section built around two or three qualities each shown through a story, a touch of the person's own words or humor, and a short closing about what they gave you and a simple goodbye.

**What should you not say in a eulogy?**
Avoid reciting the whole life in order, listing accomplishments like a résumé, sharing grievances or too-private inside jokes, and leaning on generic clichés. Specific, true, and warm always lands better than grand and impersonal.
