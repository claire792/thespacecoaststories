A legacy book comes together in five stages: the conversations, the recording and transcription, the writing and shaping, the design with photographs, and the printing. Start to finish, it usually takes a few weeks to a few months, depending on the length and the life. Here's a look behind the scenes at how a whole life becomes a book you can hold.

People often ask me how this actually works. What happens after they reach out? How does a stack of memories in someone's head end up as a bound book on the shelf? It's less mysterious than it sounds, and honestly, it's my favorite thing to talk about.

Let me walk you through it, the way I'd walk your family through it.

## Stage one: the conversations

Everything starts with talking. I sit down with your loved one, usually over several relaxed sessions, and ask the questions that open them up. Not a rigid script. A real conversation that follows where they want to go.

This is the part families are often surprised by. The person they thought was private, who "never talks about the old days," opens right up once someone genuinely wants to listen and there's no pressure. Some of my favorite stories have come an hour into a session, out of nowhere, from someone who swore they had nothing to tell.

## Stage two: recording and transcription

Every conversation is recorded, so nothing gets lost and I can look you in the eye instead of scribbling notes. Afterward, those recordings become text, word for word.

That transcript is the raw gold. It's got their phrasing in it, their expressions, the way they actually say things. Protecting that voice is the whole job, and it starts here.

## Stage three: writing and shaping

This is the craft part, and where a personal historian earns their keep. A life doesn't come out in order or in tidy chapters. It comes out in pieces, out of sequence, tangled up.

My work is to shape all of that into a story that flows and holds together, without sanding off what makes it theirs. When it's done right, your family reads it and hears the person, not me. That's the line I'm always walking: make it readable, keep it *them*.

## Stage four: design and photographs

Then the book becomes a book to look at, not just read. Old photos get gathered and, where they need it, gently restored. Each one gets placed alongside the part of the story it belongs to, so the picture and the memory sit together.

We settle the details that make it feel finished: the cover, the layout, the title. This is the stage where families often get emotional, because for the first time they're seeing it take real shape.

## Stage five: printing and holding it

Last, it's printed. Softcover or hardcover, one copy or a whole set for the family. And there's a moment, every time, when the person whose life it is opens the finished book and goes quiet.

That moment is why I do this.

## How long it takes, and what you do

Most projects run a few weeks to a few months, depending on how long the life is and how many copies you want. Longer books and larger families take more time.

Your part is small: help me get access to your loved one and the old photos, answer a few fact-checking questions along the way, and then get out of the way and let the stories happen. I handle the rest.

## What makes it worth it

I've watched a lot of families open that final book. I've seen grandchildren read it and finally understand where they come from. I've seen it become the thing everyone reaches for after the person is gone, the closest thing to having them back in the room.

Flowers wilt. Gadgets break. This is one of the few things you can make that gets more precious with every year and every generation.

## Frequently asked questions

**How is a legacy book made?**
In five stages: recorded conversations with your loved one, transcription of those recordings, writing that shapes the memories into a flowing story in their voice, design that pairs photographs with the text, and printing into a finished book. A personal historian manages the whole process.

**How long does it take to make a legacy book?**
Usually a few weeks to a few months, depending on the length of the book, how many interview sessions it takes, and how many copies you're printing. Longer, more detailed life stories and larger print runs take more time.

**How many interviews does a legacy book take?**
It varies with the scope, but most projects involve several relaxed sessions rather than one long one. Shorter sessions spread out produce warmer, richer material and are easier on your loved one.

**What do I have to do as the family?**
Not much. You help arrange access to your loved one and gather old photographs, answer occasional fact-checking questions, and let the conversations unfold. The interviewing, writing, design, and printing are handled for you.
