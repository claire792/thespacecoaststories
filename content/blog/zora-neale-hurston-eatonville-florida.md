*Published July 3, 2026 · Updated July 3, 2026*

Zora Neale Hurston was a Florida writer, anthropologist, and folklorist who grew up in Eatonville, one of the first incorporated all-Black towns in America. She spent her life traveling the South collecting ordinary people's folklore, songs, and spoken stories before they could be lost, the exact work a personal historian does today. Here's her remarkable Florida story.

Of everyone in this series, Zora Neale Hurston is the one I feel closest to. Long before anyone used the words "personal historian," she was doing the work: sitting with ordinary people, listening hard, and writing down their stories so they wouldn't disappear.

She's a kindred spirit, and one of Florida's greatest.

## The short version

- **January 7, 1891** — Born in Notasulga, Alabama
- **1894** — Moves as a small child to Eatonville, Florida, one of the first incorporated all-Black towns in the U.S.
- **1918–1928** — Studies at Howard University, then Barnard College, where she earns a degree in anthropology
- **1927–1932** — Travels the South and Caribbean collecting folklore and oral stories
- **1935** — Publishes *Mules and Men*, a landmark collection of African-American folklore
- **1937** — Publishes her masterpiece novel, *Their Eyes Were Watching God*
- **January 28, 1960** — Dies in Fort Pierce, Florida, in poverty and obscurity

## Three turning points

**Growing up in Eatonville.** Hurston came of age in a self-governing all-Black town, surrounded by people telling stories on porches and in stores. That town, and its rich spoken culture, became the wellspring of everything she wrote. She grew up understanding that ordinary people's talk was worth treasure.

**Studying anthropology, then coming home to listen.** At Barnard and Columbia, Hurston trained as an anthropologist under the famous Franz Boas. Then she turned that training on her own people, traveling the South with a notebook and, later, a recorder, gathering the folktales, songs, and sayings of everyday Black Americans before they vanished. She gave academic weight to stories the wider world had ignored.

**Writing *Their Eyes Were Watching God*.** In 1937 she poured that deep listening into a novel that's now considered an American classic. It reads like the real voices she'd spent her life collecting, because it was built from them.

## How Florida shaped her

Hurston is Florida to the core. Eatonville made her, and Florida's people filled her notebooks and her books. She understood something the whole state can teach: that the richest stories aren't in the halls of the powerful, but on ordinary porches, in ordinary voices, in the places people call home.

## What her story teaches us about our own

This is the heart of it, and it's why I wanted her in this series.

Zora Neale Hurston built her entire life around a single conviction: that ordinary people's stories matter, and that if no one records them, they're lost forever. She spent years making sure other people's voices survived.

And here's the ache of it. Her own story nearly didn't survive. She died in poverty and was buried in an unmarked grave, largely forgotten, until the writer Alice Walker went searching, found the grave, and marked it in 1973, sparking the revival that made Hurston famous again. The woman who saved everyone else's stories almost lost her own.

Let that be the lesson. Even a great chronicler of stories can be forgotten if no one preserves *hers*. The ordinary voices in your own family, on your own porches, are exactly the treasure Hurston spent her life protecting. Don't wait for an Alice Walker to come looking. Write them down now, while the people are still here to tell them.

## Frequently asked questions

**Where did Zora Neale Hurston grow up?**
Hurston grew up in Eatonville, Florida, one of the first incorporated all-Black towns in the United States. She was born in Notasulga, Alabama, in 1891 and moved to Eatonville as a small child. The town deeply shaped her writing.

**What did Zora Neale Hurston do?**
She was a writer, anthropologist, and folklorist who traveled the American South and Caribbean collecting African-American folklore, songs, and oral stories. She's best known for the novel *Their Eyes Were Watching God* (1937) and the folklore collection *Mules and Men* (1935).

**Why was Zora Neale Hurston's grave unmarked?**
Hurston died in poverty and relative obscurity in 1960 and was buried in an unmarked grave in Fort Pierce, Florida. The writer Alice Walker located and marked it in 1973, helping spark a major revival of interest in Hurston's work.
