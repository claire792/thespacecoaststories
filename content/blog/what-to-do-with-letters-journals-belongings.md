When sorting a loved one's letters, journals, and belongings, go slowly and resist discarding anything meaningful in the early rush of grief. Set aside the writings, photos, and objects that carry their voice or story, digitize and preserve the most precious, and consider turning letters and journals into a keepsake the family can share. Here's a gentle, practical guide.

After a loss, there comes a strange and tender task: deciding what to do with everything they left behind. The clothes, the papers, the boxes of letters, the journal you didn't know they kept. It can feel overwhelming, and it can feel like touching them again. Both are true.

There's no rush and no single right way. But a little guidance helps, so here it is.

## First, give yourself time

The most important rule: don't make big decisions in the first raw weeks if you can help it.

Grief clouds judgment, and things thrown out in those early days can't be gotten back. If family is pressing to clear a house quickly, at minimum set aside all papers, photos, letters, and journals into boxes to sort later, when you can breathe. The rest can wait. You are allowed to take your time.

## What to keep

You don't have to keep everything, and you shouldn't. But some things carry the person in a way that's worth protecting:

- **Anything in their handwriting.** Letters, journals, notes in margins, recipe cards, the grocery list on the fridge. Their hand is their voice on paper.
- **Photographs**, especially labeled ones.
- **Recordings**, voicemails, home videos, anything with their voice.
- **A few meaningful objects.** Not everything, but the pocket watch, the apron, the tool, the thing that was *them*.
- **Documents** that tell family history: certificates, military papers, immigration records.

For the photos specifically, our guide on [organizing old family photos](/blog/how-to-organize-old-family-photos/) will help.

## How to preserve letters and journals

Paper is fragile, and it's often the most precious thing left. Protect it.

1. **Handle gently**, with clean, dry hands.
2. **Store flat**, out of heat, sunlight, and humidity, which matters especially here in Florida. Acid-free boxes are ideal.
3. **Digitize it.** Scan or photograph letters and journal pages so the words survive even if the paper doesn't, and so family can share them.
4. **Back up** the digital copies in more than one place.

## What to do with the stories inside

Here's the part that turns grief into something lasting. Those letters and journals aren't just objects. They're your loved one's voice, their thoughts, their story, in their own words.

Many families find real comfort in gathering them into something to keep and share: transcribing a journal, collecting the letters into a bound book, or weaving the writings together with photos into a keepsake the whole family can hold. Instead of a box in one person's closet, their words become something everyone can return to. That's often where a [tribute book](/blog/how-to-create-a-tribute-book/) begins.

## Handling the hard emotions

Some days sorting will feel like a gift, like spending an afternoon with them. Some days it will be too much. Stop when it's too much. Come back when you can.

Do it with family when you can, so the memories get shared and the load is lighter. And if you find something painful or surprising, be gentle with yourself and with their memory. A whole person lived here. Let there be room for all of them.

## Sharing with family

Once you've preserved things, share them. Send scans of the letters to siblings and cousins. Make copies of the photos. A story or a keepsake grows more precious the more hands hold it, and it keeps your loved one present in the whole family, not just in one closet.

## Frequently asked questions

**What should I do with a deceased loved one's letters and journals?**
Go slowly and don't discard them in early grief. Store them safely away from heat and humidity, digitize them by scanning or photographing so the words survive, and consider gathering them into a keepsake book to share with family. Their writing is their voice preserved.

**How do I decide what belongings to keep after someone dies?**
Keep anything in their handwriting, photographs, recordings of their voice, key family documents, and a few objects that truly capture who they were. You don't need to keep everything. When in doubt, box items to decide later rather than discarding during the raw early weeks.

**How can I preserve old letters and journals?**
Handle them with clean, dry hands, store them flat in acid-free boxes away from sunlight, heat, and humidity, and digitize them by scanning or photographing. Back up the digital copies in more than one place so the words are protected even if the paper degrades.

**What can I do with a loved one's writings to honor them?**
Gather their letters and journal entries into something lasting: transcribe a journal, collect letters into a bound book, or combine their writings with photos into a keepsake. This turns scattered papers into a shared family treasure in their own words.
