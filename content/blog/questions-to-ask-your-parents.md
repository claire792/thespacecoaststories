Most people who want to capture a parent's stories get stuck in the same place: they don't know what to ask.

So they ask something big and vague. *Tell me about your life.* And the parent freezes, waves it off, says there's nothing to tell. The conversation dies before it starts.

The fix is smaller, stranger, more specific questions. Not "what was your childhood like," but "what did your street sound like on a summer night." Specific questions pull up specific memories, and specific memories are where the real stories live.

Here are twenty that work. Steal them.

## The warm-up

1. What's the earliest thing you can remember?
2. Tell me about the house you grew up in. Start at the front door and walk me through it.
3. What did a Sunday look like when you were a kid?
4. Who did you spend the most time with growing up, and what did you get up to?
5. What did your family worry about money-wise, and did you know it at the time?

## The turning points

6. What's the bravest thing you ever did?
7. Was there a moment you knew your whole life had just changed?
8. What's a chance you took that worked out? One that didn't?
9. Tell me about the day you met the person you married, or the day you knew.
10. What's the hardest year you've lived through, and how did you get out the other side?

## The inner life

11. What did you believe at twenty that you'd argue with now?
12. Who did you love before you understood what love was?
13. What are you proudest of that almost nobody knows about?
14. Is there something you've never told me that you'd want me to know?
15. What do you wish you'd asked *your* parents?

## The everyday, which is secretly the best stuff

16. What did our kitchen smell like when you were cooking?
17. What was your first car, and what happened in it?
18. What song takes you straight back, and to where?
19. What's a rule your parents had that you swore you'd never repeat, and did you?
20. When you're gone, what do you hope we still do the way you taught us?

## A few tips for the actual conversation

**Record it.** Use your phone. You cannot both listen well and write fast, and you'll want the exact words later. Ask permission, set the phone down, forget about it.

**Ask one question and then be quiet.** The temptation is to fill silence. Don't. The best answers come three or four seconds after you think the answer's finished.

**Don't correct them.** If Dad's version of a story doesn't match the family record, let it ride. You're capturing how *he* remembers it, and that's the point.

**Do it in small doses.** Twenty minutes at a time beats one exhausting marathon. Regular short sessions get you further, and they're easier on everyone.

**Print what you get.** A voice memo lost in your phone isn't a keepsake. Even a few transcribed pages, tucked in a folder, is something your family can actually hold.
