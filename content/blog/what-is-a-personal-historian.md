A personal historian is a professional who preserves people's life stories, usually by interviewing them and shaping those conversations into a book, an audio recording, or a video. Think of it as a biographer for everyday families, not just the famous. Here's what a personal historian actually does, how they're different from a genealogist, what it costs, and how to tell if your family needs one.

Most people have never heard the title, and then they hear it and think, oh, that's a thing? It is. There's a whole quiet profession of writers and interviewers whose entire job is making sure ordinary people's stories don't disappear. I'm one of them.

Let me explain what we do, in plain terms.

## What a personal historian does

At the heart of it, a personal historian asks and listens, then turns what they hear into something you can keep.

The work usually looks like this:

1. **Interviews.** Sitting down with your loved one over several sessions, asking the right questions, and drawing out the real stories, including the ones they'd never think to volunteer.
2. **Recording and transcribing.** Capturing it all as audio, then turning it into text.
3. **Writing and shaping.** Building the raw memories into a story that flows and, crucially, still sounds like the person. Not a report. Them.
4. **Design and photos.** Laying it out, restoring and placing old photographs where they belong.
5. **Producing the final thing.** Most often a printed book, sometimes an audio or video piece, made to last and to be shared.

Some personal historians specialize, in memoirs, in video, in businesses or organizations. What we share is the mission: catch the story while the person is still here to tell it.

## How a personal historian is different from a genealogist

People mix these up constantly, so here's the clean distinction.

A **genealogist** researches your family tree. They chase names, dates, records, and bloodlines, often reaching back centuries to find who your ancestors were.

A **personal historian** captures the living story. We're less interested in the branches of the tree and more interested in what your grandmother remembers, how she felt, what her voice sounds like telling it. A genealogist can tell you your great-grandfather emigrated in 1912. A personal historian sits with your grandfather and records what it was actually like the day he arrived.

You might want both. But if your worry is "I don't want to lose Mom's stories," that's a personal historian.

## Who hires a personal historian

Families feeling the clock, most of all. An aging parent, a grandparent whose memory is starting to slip, a relative facing a hard diagnosis. The common thread is the sense that a window is closing.

Also: people marking a milestone, an eightieth birthday, a fiftieth anniversary. Families who've realized they don't actually know their own history. And those who've just lost someone and want to gather what remains before it scatters.

And often, honestly, the adult child who tried to do it themselves and found it was harder than expected, either the recording and writing, or the simple fact that Mom won't open up to her own daughter the way she will to a warm stranger.

## What does it cost, and is it worth it

It ranges widely depending on length, interview hours, and whether you want a simple keepsake or a full hardcover. I wrote a whole plain-English breakdown in [how much it costs to hire a memoir writer](/blog/cost-to-hire-memoir-writer).

Is it worth it? I'm biased, so weigh that. But this is one of the few things you can pay for that your family will still be holding in fifty years. Most purchases lose value the moment you unwrap them. This one gains it.

## How to know if your family needs one

Ask yourself a simple question: if the oldest person in your family passed tomorrow, how much of their story would go with them? If the answer makes your stomach drop, that's your answer.

## Frequently asked questions

**What does a personal historian do?**
A personal historian preserves someone's life story by interviewing them, recording and transcribing the conversations, and shaping the material into a finished book, audio, or video that keeps their voice and history for the family. It's biography work for everyday people rather than celebrities.

**What's the difference between a personal historian and a genealogist?**
A genealogist researches your family tree, tracing names, dates, and ancestry through records. A personal historian captures a living person's memories and stories in their own words. Genealogy is about who your ancestors were; personal history is about what your loved one actually remembers and sounds like.

**How much does a personal historian cost?**
It varies with the length of the project, the number of interview hours, and whether you want a simple keepsake or a full hardcover book. It can range from a few hundred dollars to several thousand. The main drivers are interview time, writing, design, and printing.

**Do I really need a personal historian, or can I do it myself?**
You can absolutely do it yourself, and many families do. Hiring one makes sense when time is short, when a loved one opens up more to a neutral outsider than to family, or when you want a polished, finished book without doing the writing and design. Even capturing the interviews now protects the story either way.
