A tribute book is a printed keepsake that gathers a loved one's life story, their photographs, and the memories of the people who knew them, created after they've passed. To make one, collect their story and pictures, invite family and friends to share their memories, shape it all into chapters, and have it printed. Here's how to begin, even while you're still grieving.

First, I'm sorry. If you're reading this, you've lost someone, and you're trying to hold onto them in the one way that's still open to you. That instinct is a good one. The stories are what's left, and they're worth keeping.

Take this at your own pace. There's no deadline on a tribute book, no matter what anyone tells you. Some families start within weeks, while the memories are close. Others wait a year, until they can look at the photos without coming apart. Both are right. You'll know when you're ready.

## What a tribute book actually is

It's not a funeral program and it's not a sympathy card. It's a real, bound book about a real person, made to be read and reread by the people who loved them.

Some are short, just a gathering of favorite stories and pictures. Others tell the whole life, start to finish. There's no wrong size. What matters is that it sounds like them and brings them back, even for a few pages at a time.

## How to create one, step by step

1. **Start gathering, not writing.** Before anything else, collect what already exists. Photos, letters, a recorded voicemail you can't bring yourself to delete, the eulogy, old cards. Put it all in one place, digital or a real box on the table.

2. **Write down what you remember first.** Your own memories are the most fragile right now, because grief blurs things. Sit down and get your stories on paper before they soften. Don't edit. Just get them down.

3. **Invite others in.** This is the part that heals. Reach out to family and friends and ask each of them for one memory, one story, one thing they never want to forget. People who couldn't find words at the service often write something beautiful a month later.

4. **Find the shape.** Once the pieces are in, a natural order usually appears. Sometimes it's their life in order, childhood forward. Sometimes it's grouped by the parts of them, the parent, the friend, the fisherman, the cook. Let the material tell you.

5. **Place the photographs with the stories.** A picture of your dad at the lake means more sitting next to the story of the summers he took you there. Pair them up.

6. **Have it printed to keep.** A bound book survives what a folder of files won't. Print enough copies that everyone who loved them can hold one.

## What to put in it

The obvious things belong: the big moments, the milestones, the photos everyone knows.

But make room for the small things, because those are what people ache for later. The way they answered the phone. Their terrible favorite joke. What they always ordered. The saying they wore out. These ordinary details are what make a reader hear the person's voice again, and that's the whole point.

Their own words, if you have them. A letter they wrote, a recording, a note in the margin of a book. Anything in their actual voice is gold.

## Doing this while you're grieving

Be gentle with yourself. Working on a tribute book can feel like sitting with the person, and some days that's a comfort and some days it's too much. Stop when it's too much. Come back when you can.

It helps to make it a shared task. Grief is lighter when it's carried together, and asking your family for their memories gives everyone something to do with the love that has nowhere to go right now.

## Frequently asked questions

**What is a tribute book?**
A tribute book is a printed keepsake that honors someone who has passed by collecting their life story, photographs, and the memories of the people who knew them into one bound volume. It's more personal and lasting than a funeral program or memorial card.

**How soon after a death should I make a tribute book?**
There's no set timeline. Some families begin within weeks while memories are fresh; others wait months or a year until it feels manageable. Both are fine. The one thing worth doing early is asking family and friends to write down their memories before those details fade.

**How do I collect memories from family and friends for a memorial book?**
Reach out personally and ask each person for one specific memory, story, or detail they never want to forget. Give them a simple way to send it, by text, email, or handwriting. People who were too grief-stricken to speak at the service often write something moving in the weeks after.

**Can someone create a tribute book for me?**
Yes. A personal historian can gather the photos and stories, interview the family, write and design the book, and manage the printing, so you end up with a finished keepsake without having to assemble it yourself during a hard season.
