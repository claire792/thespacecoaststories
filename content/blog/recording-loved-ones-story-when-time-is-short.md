When time is short, focus on capturing a loved one's voice and the few stories that matter most to them, not a complete life history. Keep sessions brief and gentle, follow their energy, ask what they most want remembered, and record it as audio or video. Even a short recording becomes something your family treasures forever. Here's a compassionate guide for families in hospice or facing a terminal illness.

If you're reading this, you're in one of the hardest places there is: loving someone whose time is limited, and wanting to hold onto them. I'm so sorry. And I want you to know that what you're thinking of doing, capturing their stories now, is a gift to your whole family, and often to the person themselves.

Let me help you do it in a way that's gentle for everyone.

## Why this matters, even now

There's real comfort in it, and not just for those left behind.

Reflecting on one's life near its end is a recognized source of meaning and peace. Palliative care has a practice called **life review**, and a well-studied therapy called **dignity therapy**, both built on a simple truth: helping someone tell the story of their life, and know it mattered, can ease distress and restore a sense of dignity at the end. When you sit and ask your loved one about their life, you're not just collecting stories. You're telling them their life was worth remembering. That lands deeply.

## What to focus on when time is limited

You won't capture everything, and you don't need to. Prioritize.

1. **Their voice.** Even a few minutes of them simply talking is priceless. The sound of a laugh, a saying, a term of endearment. Get that first.
2. **What they most want remembered.** Ask them directly: "What do you want us to always remember?" Let them steer.
3. **Messages to specific people.** A few words to a grandchild, a spouse, a child. These become treasures beyond measure.
4. **The stories they light up telling.** Follow their joy, not a checklist.

Everything else is a bonus. These four are the heart.

## How to do it gently

The rules are different here than for a healthy relative. Comfort comes first, always.

- **Keep sessions short.** Ten or fifteen minutes may be all they have in them. That's plenty. Several tiny sessions beat one that exhausts them.
- **Follow their energy.** Some days there won't be a session, and that's okay. Just being there is enough on those days.
- **Let them lead.** Don't push for a story they don't want to tell, or correct their memories. Meet them where they are.
- **Record simply.** Your phone's voice or video recorder is perfect. Set it nearby and let it run. Don't make it a production.
- **It's okay if it's just presence.** If the stories don't come, holding their hand is its own kind of remembering. Release yourself from pressure.

## A few gentle questions that matter most

If you have an opening, these tend to bring the meaningful answers:

- What are you most proud of?
- What are your happiest memories?
- What do you want us to know?
- What are you grateful for?
- Is there anything you want to make sure gets said?
- What do you hope for us?

Ask one, then just listen. Let the silences be.

## Working with hospice

If your loved one is in hospice care, the team can be a real ally in this. Chaplains and social workers often understand life review deeply and can help create the right moment, or simply give you permission to do it. Don't hesitate to tell them you'd like to capture some stories. Many will welcome and support it.

## When you'd like help

Sometimes the emotion is too much to also be the interviewer, or you want it done well while you simply get to be present as their child or spouse. A personal historian experienced with end-of-life work can step in gently, follow your loved one's pace, and capture their voice and stories with care, so nothing precious is lost. ## Frequently asked questions

**How do I record a dying loved one's stories?**
Focus on capturing their voice and the few stories that matter most, keep sessions very short, follow their energy, and record simply on a phone. Ask what they most want remembered and let them lead. Even a short recording becomes a lasting treasure for your family.

**Is it too late to capture my parent's story if they're in hospice?**
Often it isn't. Even brief conversations can preserve their voice, a message to loved ones, and the stories they most want remembered. Keep it gentle and short, prioritize comfort, and let any recording, however small, be enough.

**Is talking about their life good for someone who is dying?**
It can be. Palliative care uses practices like life review and dignity therapy because reflecting on one's life and feeling it mattered can bring meaning and ease distress near the end. Always follow the person's lead and comfort, but many find real peace in being asked and heard.

**Should I ask hospice for help capturing my loved one's story?**
Yes. Hospice chaplains and social workers often understand life review and can help create the right moment or support your efforts. Let them know you'd like to capture some stories, and they can be a valuable ally.
