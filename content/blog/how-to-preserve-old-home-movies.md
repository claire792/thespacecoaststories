*Published July 13, 2026*

To preserve old home movies, VHS tapes, film reels, and slides, digitize them as soon as possible, because these formats degrade over time and the machines to play them are disappearing. You can do it yourself with a scanner or capture device, or send them to a digitizing service. Then back up the files in more than one place. Here's how, and why waiting is the real risk.

Somewhere in your house there's probably a box of VHS tapes, a few film reels, or a carousel of slides, holding your family at ages you'll never see again. And here's the hard truth: those formats are dying, right now, quietly, in that box.

The good news is you can save them. Let me walk you through it.

## Why this is urgent

This isn't a someday project, and here's why.

Magnetic tape like VHS breaks down over time. Colors fade, images get muddy, and tapes can grow mold or simply stop playing. Film gets brittle. And just as bad, the machines are vanishing. Finding a working VCR, slide projector, or film player gets harder every year, and once you can't play a tape, you can't save what's on it.

Every year you wait, you lose a little more. That's the whole case for doing this now.

## Your options

**Do it yourself.** You can buy a device to help:
- A **VHS-to-digital capture device** connects a VCR to your computer.
- A **flatbed or dedicated photo/slide scanner** digitizes slides and prints.
- A **film scanner** handles 8mm or Super 8 reels (this one is fiddly; a service is often better).

**Use a digitizing service.** Companies will take your tapes, film, and slides and return them digitized, often on a drive or in the cloud. It costs more, but it's easier and usually gives better results, especially for delicate film. Local camera shops sometimes offer this too.

## Step by step

1. **Gather it all** into one place: tapes, reels, slides, discs.
2. **Label what you can** before you start: dates, events, who's in them.
3. **Handle gently.** Keep everything cool, dry, and out of humidity, which matters double here in Florida. Heat and damp are what kill these formats fastest.
4. **Digitize**, yourself or through a service. Start with the oldest and most fragile items first.
5. **Save in a common format** (MP4 for video, JPEG or TIFF for images) so files stay playable for years.
6. **Back up in at least two places**, such as an external drive and the cloud. One copy is not preserved. It's just waiting to be lost.
7. **Share with family.** Send copies to relatives. Spread out, the memories are far safer.

## What to do once they're digitized

Here's where it gets meaningful. Once those old movies and slides are digital, they become usable again. You can watch them, share them, and pair them with the stories behind them.

The richest thing you can do is sit down with older relatives, watch the footage together, and record them telling you what's happening, who everyone is, and what they remember. The film shows the moment; your relative's voice explains it. Together, that's a family treasure no degrading tape could ever hold. It's also often where a [legacy book or family history project](/blog/how-to-make-a-family-history-book) begins.

## Frequently asked questions

**How do I preserve old VHS tapes?**
Digitize them as soon as possible using a VHS-to-digital capture device with a working VCR, or send them to a digitizing service. VHS tape degrades over time and VCRs are disappearing, so converting them to digital files, then backing those up in two places, is the way to preserve them.

**Do old home movies and tapes really degrade?**
Yes. Magnetic tape like VHS loses quality over time and can grow mold or become unplayable, and film becomes brittle. The equipment to play these formats is also getting rare. Digitizing them soon is the only reliable way to save them.

**Should I digitize old movies myself or use a service?**
Do it yourself with a capture device or scanner if you're comfortable and have many items; use a professional service for delicate film, large batches, or the best quality. Film reels in particular are often better left to a service.

**What should I do with digitized home movies?**
Back them up in at least two places, share copies with family, and, most meaningfully, watch them with older relatives while recording them explaining who and what is on screen. Pairing the footage with the stories behind it turns raw video into a lasting family history.
